Executive Summary

Residential Roofing Protects the Entire Home

A residential roof protects the structure, attic, insulation, ceilings, walls, electrical systems, finishes, and belongings inside the home. Roof performance depends on more than the visible roofing material.

The complete system may include decking, underlayment, ice-and-water membrane, starter strips, field shingles or specialty roofing, valleys, step flashing, counterflashing, drip edge, ridge materials, ventilation, gutters, skylights, chimneys, pipe boots, and low-slope roof sections.

Maximum Roofing helps homeowners determine whether the roof needs a targeted repair, maintenance, partial replacement, specialty-material work, or full replacement. Recommendations are based on roof condition, moisture, storm exposure, material type, age, ventilation, decking, flashing, and long-term goals.

Specialty Roofing

Slate Roofing

Natural slate is a stone roofing material valued for its appearance, durability, and long service potential when the complete roof system is properly designed, installed, and maintained.

Slate roofing requires careful material handling, appropriate fastening, proper headlap, durable underlayment, well-designed valleys, and reliable flashing. Roof structure and deck condition should be evaluated because slate is heavier than many common roofing materials.

Slate Roof Installation

New slate installation, replacement planning, layout, fastening, underlayment, valleys, ridges, and flashing.

Slate Roof Repair

Broken or slipped slate replacement, flashing repairs, localized underlayment concerns, and storm-damage evaluation.

Historic and Detailed Roofs

Complex roofs may include dormers, turrets, copper work, masonry transitions, decorative ridges, and specialty details.

Important: Walking directly on slate can crack tiles. Slate inspection and repair should use methods appropriate for brittle, layered stone roofing.

Natural Wood Roofing

Cedar Shake and Cedar Shingle Roofing

Cedar roofing provides a natural wood appearance and is available in different grades, cuts, thicknesses, and profiles. Cedar shakes are typically more textured, while cedar shingles are generally more uniform.

Cedar performance depends on material quality, exposure, spacing, fastening, ventilation, underlayment, roof slope, flashing, moisture conditions, and maintenance. Fire-treatment or code requirements may apply depending on the product and location.

Cedar Installation

New cedar shake or shingle installation with appropriate spacing, exposure, fasteners, underlayment, and flashing.

Cedar Repair

Split, missing, curled, decayed, or storm-damaged pieces, flashing repairs, and localized replacement.

Cedar Maintenance

Inspection for moisture retention, debris, moss, algae, splitting, fastener issues, ventilation, and adjacent tree conditions.

Important: Cedar should not be sealed with incompatible products that trap moisture. Product and maintenance choices should fit the specific cedar system.

Attic Performance

Attic Ventilation and Moisture Control

Attic ventilation helps manage heat and moisture when the roof and building are designed for a vented attic. Intake and exhaust should work together, and insulation should not block required airflow.

Poor ventilation, air leakage from the home, insufficient insulation, bathroom exhaust, interior humidity, and roof leaks can all contribute to condensation or moisture. Roofing work should not assume that every attic moisture problem is caused by exterior leakage.

  • Soffit or low-level intake ventilation
  • Ridge, box, or other exhaust ventilation
  • Blocked ventilation paths
  • Interior air leakage
  • Bathroom and kitchen exhaust routing
  • Insulation depth and placement
Weather Damage

Residential Storm and Wind Damage

Storms can remove shingles, crack slate, split cedar, damage metal, puncture low-slope membranes, loosen flashing, bend gutters, and cause branches or debris to strike the roof.

Some wind damage is hidden. Materials may remain in place while seals, fasteners, edges, or flashing are weakened. A post-storm inspection can help identify conditions before the next rainfall.

Maximum Roofing can document visible conditions and repair recommendations. Insurance coverage is determined by the policy and carrier.

Check After Storms

Control Moss and Debris

Keep Records

Photos, invoices, warranty documents, inspections, and repair history support future planning.

Residential Roofing Warranty Guide

Manufacturer Material Warranty

Material warranties address qualifying product defects under written terms. Coverage varies by manufacturer, material, product line, registration, installation requirements, and exclusions.

Enhanced Manufacturer Warranty

Some systems may qualify for enhanced coverage when approved components, installer requirements, ventilation, registration, and other conditions are satisfied.

Contractor Workmanship Warranty

A workmanship warranty addresses the contractor's installation work under the written agreement and is separate from manufacturer coverage.
